Course Description

This course is an investigation of genres of professional and workplace communication that are common across the business world, including memos, reports, and presentations.

We will practice writing in these genres this semester. However, our ultimate goal will not be to learn a particular handful of genres, but to develop practical strategies for writing in any new genres we may encounter in our future professional lives. These include:

  1. Developing clarity and concision at the sentence level
  2. Analyzing the conventions and document design of particular genres
  3. Developing writing relationships with others – which includes learning to give helpful feedback and to incorporate received feedback into the revision of your writing.

Being able to adapt to new forms of communication in the workplace is important because, as you all well know, communication in general is changing rapidly. Contemporary professional communication occurs across media platforms and through a variety of devices. Consequently, this course addresses a range of digital and visual communication strategies.

Learning Outcomes

The following learning outcomes must be met:

  1. Compose in academic, professional, and/or workplaces genres related to a field of study.
  2. Apply writing processes common to the field.
  3. Compose and deliver a professional presentation.
  4. Describe the conventions of genres within a field.
  5. Make effective disciplinary and professional arguments.

Assignments

Cover Letter: Cover letters are a way to communicate your suitability for a position to potential employers. You will write one to me, explaining your prior knowledge of and experience with writing professionally, and “pitching” your suitability for this course.

Memos: With the emergence of email, this genre has become out-of-date, but it is the form of workplace communication email was modeled after. Studying memos will afford us an opportunity to consider how genres change over time, as well as how older genres continue to influence current ones. They are also a simple way to start considering how documents are designed.

Usability Study: Usability studies, as the name implies, are documents that attempt to account for how usable something is. Whether that “something” is a website, a car, or a surgical tool, usability studies provide information about how it is used — does it function as intended? is it approachable? is it deployed in surprising ways? In a small group, you will conduct a usability study and document your findings. In addition to the written document, you will present your findings in a group presentation. You will also file a progress report to me along the way, informing me about how your group project is going, and what successes and difficulties you have encountered along the way.

Marketing Report: How do companies use social media? Acting as a consultant for an imaginary corporation, you will prepare a report documenting some best practices for their social media campaigns. You will research how corporate social media accounts develop their “brand,” both in the field (by observing what real social media accounts post) and theoretically (by consulting secondary research on social media management).

UB Portfolio Submission: If you are completing this course as part of your UB Curriculum requirements, please select an “artifact” from this course that is representative of your learning and save it in a safe location with a clear title. Your final UB Curriculum requirement, UBC 399: UB Curriculum Capstone, will require you to submit these “artifacts” as you process and reflect on your achievement and growth through the UB Curriculum. Artifacts include homework assignments, exams, research papers, projects, lab reports, presentations, and other coursework. For more information, see the UB Curriculum Capstone website: https://www.buffalo.edu/ubcurriculum/capstone.html.

Peer Reviewing: All major assignments in this course are subject to peer review. You will be assigned to a small group with your fellow students, and you will read each other's work throughout the semester, offering suggestions to each other on how to improve each other's writing. You should read your peers' writing like you read anything else in this class — carefully, thoughtfully, taking notes. Your feedback should be extensive, engaged, and critical where necessary. We will discuss strategies for providing excellent feedback more in class.

Participation: Participation is a significant component of this course. Though it is certainly more natural to have active, engaged conversations when we occupy the same room, it is still vitally important for your own success and our success as a class that you speak up, and often. As in the professional world, you have to get noticed to get ahead here. It is expected that you come to class having read what was assigned for the day, that you voice your opinions on what you have read, and that you respond in a kind, thoughtful manner to the insights of your peers.

Policies

Attendance Policy

You may be absent from class without an excuse three times this semester without penalty to your grade. After that, each additional unexcused absence will lower your grade by one-third of a letter. If you need to be excused from class for the day, you must inform me in advance.

Late Work

If you need an extension on a paper, you must inform me in advance. Late work will not be accepted without an extension.

Drafting

All writing in this class is the product of drafting and revision. If you fail to turn in a draft on time, you will not receive comments from your peers. A final draft that is not the product of a peer-review/revision cycle will be graded one letter lower than the quality of the work.

Academic Dishonesty

Please familiarize yourself with the University’s Academic Integrity policy, which can be found at https://catalog.buffalo.edu/policies/integrity.html. In a writing course, we are particularly concerned with plagiarism, which the catalog describes as “Copying or receiving material from any source and submitting that material as one’s own, without acknowledging and citing the particular debts to the source (quotations, paraphrases, basic ideas), or in any other manner representing the work of another as one’s own.” The writing program’s policy states that the first instance of plagiarism will result in an “F” on that assignment. Any additional instances of plagiarism will result in failure of the course.

Incompletes

An incomplete grade may only be given to students who have (1) fulfilled the attendance requirement for the course and (2) completed all but one of the written assignments.
